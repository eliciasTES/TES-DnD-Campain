# Lake Geir Trading Company Headquarters

Nestled along the eastern shore of Lake Geir, just north of Ivarstead, the headquarters appears at first glance to be a respectable lakeside trading post. A sturdy timber warehouse overlooks a broad wooden dock where barges and shallow cargo boats quietly load and unload goods throughout the day. Nearby, several covered wagons wait beside a fenced freight yard, their drivers resting while laborers move crates between the warehouse and the docks.

A tall watchtower rises above the compound, giving guards an unobstructed view of the lake, nearby roads, and the surrounding pine forests. A simple wooden palisade encloses the yard—not designed to repel an army, but enough to discourage thieves and wild animals while controlling who enters and leaves.

The compound is busy but disciplined. Laborers, caravan hands, stable workers, and merchants move with purpose, creating the illusion of a legitimate shipping company. Ledgers are checked, cargo is inspected, horses are fed, and wagons arrive according to a regular schedule. Most workers likely have no idea they are helping run a criminal enterprise.

## Exterior

- Large timber warehouse overlooking Lake Geir.
- Long wooden docks with several moored cargo barges.
- Freight yard stacked with crates, barrels, lumber, and covered wagons.
- Stable and wagon repair shed.
- Small bunkhouse for laborers and caravan guards.
- Office building used by merchants and accountants.
- Watchtower overlooking both the lake and the mountain road.
- Wooden palisade with a single guarded gate.

## Interior

The warehouse floor is crowded with stacked cargo, pulley cranes, storage racks, and narrow aisles that provide countless hiding places. A raised catwalk circles the upper walls, allowing guards to patrol above the workers while overlooking nearly every corner of the building.

Behind the main warehouse sits a modest counting office where manifests, shipping records, contracts, and payment ledgers are organized. A locked cellar beneath the office contains the operation's true records—smuggling routes, coded correspondence, blackmail payments, and the names of investors financing the rival organization.

A concealed tunnel beneath the warehouse connects to a small hidden cove on the lake where contraband can be loaded onto boats under the cover of darkness, bypassing both the main dock and passing travelers.

## Security

- Guards dressed as caravan mercenaries rather than soldiers.
- Workers naturally challenge unfamiliar faces without immediately raising an alarm.
- Cargo inspections at the main gate.
- Patrols rotate between the docks, warehouse, and office.
- A signal bell in the watchtower can alert the entire compound.
- Escape boats remain tied beneath the hidden cove in case authorities arrive.

## Atmosphere

The headquarters feels less like a bandit camp and more like a successful commercial business. Everything appears organized, profitable, and legitimate. The smell of fresh-cut timber mixes with lake water, horse leather, lamp oil, and damp rope. Workers shout shipping numbers, wagons creak through the yard, cranes lift heavy cargo overhead, and merchants argue over contracts while guards keep a watchful eye on everyone entering the compound.

Only after looking closer do the inconsistencies emerge: shipping manifests that don't match their cargo, crates bearing false merchant seals, armed guards who seem overly interested in accounting books, and doors that remain locked even from trusted employees. It is a business built on deception, where legitimacy is simply the most profitable disguise.
