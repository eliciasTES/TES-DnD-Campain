Elias stepped over a discarded garland of honey-flowers, the scent of the Festival of Love clinging heavily to the humid air. Riften was a chaos of color today; ribbons of pink and gold draped from every balcony, and the streets were choked with laughing couples and street vendors hawking candied nuts. He kept his head down, weaving through the crowd with a purposeful stride that signaled he was not there for the festivities. The brightness of the day felt abrasive against the memory of the Ratway’s damp, suffocating dark, but the Guild's instructions had been clear.

"You're moving like a man who's afraid of the sun, Venym," a voice called out.

Elias paused, glancing back to see Brynjolf leaning against a weathered stone wall, a smirk playing on his lips. The Guild operative didn't move toward him, staying in the shadow of a nearby awning. He gestured with a subtle flick of his wrist toward the city gates, where a nondescript carriage waited, its driver leaning idly against the wooden frame.

"Keep that look on your face," Brynjolf added, his voice dropping to a conspiratorial hum. "The 'weary traveler' look. It’s your best asset."

Elias shot a sharp, narrow glare at Brynjolf, his jaw tightening. The last thing he needed was a public spectacle; the Guild’s penchant for dramatic farewells was a liability. If he was spotted coordinating with the city's most notorious thieves, it would only take one observant eye for the rumors to reach Mjoll. The shield-maiden’s relentless pursuit of justice was admirable, and in his head her acceptance was an evidence that he had not fully committed to a life of being an outcast.

Brynjolf caught the look and let out a short, knowing laugh. He didn't offer a handshake or a formal goodbye, instead pushing off the wall with a fluid motion and pivoting away. He began walking in the opposite direction, his stride leisurely as he headed toward the Temple of Mara, likely intending to blend into the festival crowds or perhaps flirt with a few devotees of the Goddess of Love. He vanished behind a cluster of brightly colored tents, leaving Elias alone with the hum of the city.

The cobblestones of the fishery district were slick with a translucent film of fish scales and river silt, a stark contrast to the floral opulence of the town center. Elias moved with a practiced, rhythmic gait, keeping his shoulders hunched to minimize his silhouette. He bypassed the main thoroughfare, opting instead for the narrow alleys that smelled of brine and old nets, weaving through the labyrinth of drying racks and salt-barrels. Here, the festive noise of Mara’s celebration faded into a distant, muffled roar, replaced by the rhythmic slapping of water against the piers.

At the furthest edge of the docks, where the mist of Lake Geir clung to the pylons like a damp shroud, a small, flat-bottomed skiff bobbed in the current. Standing beside it was Sigrid. She didn't look like a conspirator in a high-stakes heist; she looked like a woman who had spent forty years fighting the wind and winning. Her face was a map of deep lines and sun-weathered skin, her eyes a piercing, pale blue that seemed to see through the facade of anyone who stepped onto her deck.

"You're late," she grunted, not looking up from the coil of rope she was tightening. "The tide doesn't wait for mercenaries, and neither do I."

"The crowds are thicker than usual," Elias replied, his voice low. He stepped onto the boat, the wood creaking under his weight. He didn't offer a greeting; in this world, silence was the only currency that didn't lose its value.

Sigrid didn’t acknowledge his explanation. She simply gave a sharp nod and pushed off from the dock with a heavy oar, the skiff gliding silently into the gray expanse of Lake Geir. As the shoreline of Riften receded, the festive colors of the city blurred into a smudge of pink and gold against the horizon, eventually swallowed by the encroaching mist. Elias sat low in the prow, his hand resting on the hilt of his blade, feeling the transition from the social chaos of the surface to the cold, calculating solitude of the mission. The water here was deep and dark, reflecting a sky that seemed to be bruising into a deep purple as the afternoon waned.

The journey toward the western outskirts was a study in oppressive silence. Sigrid navigated by instinct, weaving through the jagged fingers of rock and half-sunken piers that marked the old smuggling routes. As they approached the designated drop-off point near the foothills leading toward Ivarstead, the landscape shifted from the open water of the lake to a narrow, winding canal choked with reeds. The air grew colder, carrying the scent of pine and damp earth. Sigrid brought the boat to a halt with a sudden, jarring scrape against a muddy bank.

Elias didn't wait for the boat to settle completely before he began the transformation. While Sigrid navigated the shifting currents of the lake, he worked with a methodical, almost clinical efficiency. He stripped away his cheap linen clothing and in their place, he donned the coarse, abrasive wool of the caravan worker's clothes. The tunic was a drab, indeterminate brown, smelling faintly of old tallow and stale tobacco, designed to make a man invisible by virtue of being utterly unremarkable. He kept his gear for infiltration stored in a leather pouch strapped to his side. He rubbed a smudge of river silt across his cheekbone and tucked his hair beneath a weathered cap, effectively erasing the Imperial soldier and the Daedra hunter, replacing them with a nameless laborer.

By the time the boat groaned against the muddy bank, the transition was complete. As he stepped off the skiff, he didn't move with the coiled tension of a predator, but with the heavy, slouching gait of a man who had spent ten hours hauling crates. He looked less like a warrior and more like a man who lived for the end of his shift and a cheap mug of ale. Sigrid gave him one last, scrutinizing look, her eyes lingering on the way the rough cloth hung on his broad shoulders. She didn't smile, but there was a flicker of approval in her gaze; he had successfully shed his identity, becoming just another cog in the machinery of the lake’s trade.

He moved away from the canal, heading toward the dense tree-line that shielded the approach to the compound from the north. The transition from the humid lake air to the damp, pine-heavy scent of the forest felt like crossing an invisible border. He paused beneath the sprawling canopy of an ancient cedar, leaning against the trunk to catch his breath and survey the terrain. From this vantage point, the rival trading outpost emerged from the mist—a sprawling complex of weathered timber and corrugated iron, huddled against the shoreline like a parasite clinging to the coast.

The palisade was a wall of seasoned pine, thick enough to stop a charging mammoth and topped with jagged stakes that caught the fading light like a row of broken teeth. To any other interloper, the perimeter was an absolute boundary, a physical decree that the interior belonged to the Lake Geir Trading Company and its secrets. Elias knelt in the loam, the damp pine needles cushioning his weight as he calculated the distance to the nearest blind spot. The watchtowers were spaced with a mathematician’s precision, their guards pacing the catwalks in rhythmic intervals that left only a sliver of time for a man to move between the beams of their lanterns.

He reached into his pouch and withdrew the custom grappling hook, the cold iron feeling familiar and heavy in his grip. He didn't throw it immediately; he waited, listening to the distant cadence of the guards' boots on the watchtower planks—a rhythmic *thump-scrape, thump-scrape* that told him exactly when the lookout’s back was turned. The palisade was a daunting wall of seasoned pine, too high to scale with mere fingertips and too exposed to attempt a clumsy climb. One slip, one loud groan of shifting timber, and the alarm bell would shatter the silence of the lakefront, summoning a dozen blades to his throat before he could even blink.

With a fluid motion, Elias launched the hook. It soared through the purple twilight, the chain whistling softly before the iron teeth bit deep into the upper ledge of the wall with a muted *thud*. He hauled himself upward, his muscles straining against the rough wool of his disguise, feeling the sudden, jarring transition as he crested the wall and rolled into the shadows of the interior. He froze, pressing his back against the timber, heart hammering against his ribs. A few feet away, the main gate loomed—a bottleneck of scrutiny where every face was cataloged and every identity verified. To walk through that gate was to invite a question he couldn't answer; to stay on the wall was to be a silhouette against the sky.

He stepped away from the perimeter, adjusting the slump of his shoulders to match the exhausted gait of the laborers he now mimicked. The compound was a hive of deceptive industry. Men with soot-stained faces hauled crates of "textiles" that felt far too heavy to be cloth, and the air was thick with the smell of wet pine and salted fish. Elias merged into the flow of the yard, his eyes scanning the environment with the precision of a hawk. He saw the way the guards leaned—lazy, but observant—and the way the workers avoided eye contact, moving like ghosts through their own workplace.

As he navigated toward the docks, he spotted a group of three laborers sharing a flask of something that smelled sharply of fermented honey. They laughed low, their voices rasping, and Elias felt a strange kinship with them—men used to the grind, oblivious to the high-stakes game of shadows being played around them. He kept his head down, playing the part of the new hire arriving for the graveyard shift, his eyes scanning the buildings, trying to figure out which one held the documents he needed. The "legitimate" facade of the company was a thin veil, and he could see the cracks: the way the crates were marked with symbols that didn't match any Imperial trade route, and the unnatural tension in the guards' grip on their polearms.

"Oi, you!" a voice barked, slicing through the ambient noise of the freight yard.

Elias didn't flinch. He slowed his pace, allowing his shoulders to slump further, and turned with the slow, deliberate lethargy of a man whose spirit had been crushed by a decade of manual labor. A guard with a scarred chin and a breastplate that had seen better days was eyeing him, one hand resting on the pommel of a shortsword.

"New hand?" the guard asked, his eyes scanning Elias’s rough-spun wool tunic for a familiar mark.

"Hired at the crossroads," Elias replied, his voice a low, gravelly rasp. "Told me to report for the night shift. Didn't tell me where to go for intake."

The guard’s gaze lingered on Elias for a heartbeat too long, his eyes narrowing as if trying to place the shade of brown in his tunic. Finally, the man scoffed and jerked a thumb toward a squat, timber-framed structure on the southeast edge of the compound. "The intake kiosk is over there, by the side of the office. You’re supposed to sign the ledger and get your papers stamped before you touch a single crate. But the clerk’s a lazy drunk; he’s gone for the night. You’ll find the kiosk empty."

The guard shifted his weight, his expression turning from suspicion to a dull, professional boredom. "You’re out of luck. Head back to the main gate and see if your supervisor is waiting for you there. If he’s not, you’re probably a mistake in the payroll." He paused, his hand tightening on his sword hilt. "Actually, come on. I’ll walk you back. Too many 'lost' laborers wandering into the warehouses lately."

Elias didn't panic; he leaned into the exhaustion. He let out a long, shuddering sigh, his shoulders dropping so low he looked almost defeated. He reached into his pocket and produced a crumpled, sweat-stained piece of parchment—a blank scrap he’d prepared earlier—and held it out with a trembling hand. "Please," he muttered, his voice cracking with a desperate, weary edge. "My boots are through the soles, and I can’t walk another mile. If the supervisor isn't there, I’m just going to sleep in the hayloft until morning. Just let me check the kiosk myself—if it's empty, I'll head back. I don't want to get in trouble for being late on my first night."

The guard looked at the pathetic scrap of paper, then at the man who looked like he had been chewed up and spat out by the road. A small, cruel smile touched the guard's lips; he enjoyed the power of the moment. He decided the effort of escorting a broken man back to the gate wasn't worth the calories. "Fine. Get out of my sight. But if I catch you sleeping near the docks, I’m tossing you in the lake."

The moment the guard’s boots retreated into the rhythmic drone of the freight yard, the slump in Elias’s shoulders vanished. The defeated laborer evaporated, replaced by the predatory stillness of a man who had spent years hunting things that lived in the dark. He didn't turn back toward the gate; instead, he melted into the gap between a stack of oversized cedar planks and a row of iron-bound crates. He moved with a ghost’s cadence, stepping only when the wind whipped through the eaves of the warehouse, using the sudden gusts to mask the soft scuff of his boots on the grit.

He navigated the perimeter of the office area, treating the open spaces like minefields. He didn't trust the guard's boredom to last; in a place like this, suspicion was the primary currency, and once a man became a curiosity, he became a target. Elias clung to the jagged shadows cast by the flickering oil lamps, his body pressed tight against the rough-hewn timber of the warehouse wall. He timed his movements to the patrol's rotation, sliding across a narrow alleyway just as a guard climbed the stairs of the watchtower, the man's silhouette momentarily blotting out the moonlight.

As he neared the counting office, the smell of old ink and expensive tobacco drifted through the air—the scent of administration and greed. The office was a squat building of dark oak, its windows narrow and reinforced with iron bars. Elias paused, crouching beneath a heavy wagon wheel, his gaze fixed on the single door. He could hear the low murmur of voices inside—men discussing shipping manifests and gold—and the distant, rhythmic clatter of the pulley cranes in the main warehouse. The air here was stagnant, thick with the humidity of the lake, making the wool of his disguise feel like a wet blanket against his skin.

The oak siding of the counting office was seasoned with age and salt, leaving a narrow, jagged gap where the timber had shrunk away from the frame. Elias pressed his ear against the rough wood, shutting out the distant noise of the docks to focus on the interior. He didn't listen for words—words could be deceptive, and a conversation could be a distraction—but for the rhythm of weight. He counted the distinct thuds of leather soles against the floorboards: the heavy, uneven stomp of a man who favored his left leg, the rhythmic, confident click of a supervisor’s polished boots, and the softer, hurried shuffle of a clerk. Three men. Based on the spatial echo, two were gathered around the heavy mahogany desk, while the third paced the perimeter, likely the guard tasked with ensuring the ledgers remained undisturbed.

He couldn't linger. The patrol outside was a clockwork machine, and the window of invisibility was closing. A single misplaced shadow or a stray breath caught in the moonlight would turn him from a ghost into a target. Elias shifted his weight, feeling the dampness of the soil seep through his trousers, his mind mapping the interior of the office based on the sounds of the men's movement. The supervisor was leaning over a map, his voice a low, guttural drone that spoke of quotas and "unforeseen delays" in the western shipments.

With a sudden, sharp intake of breath, Elias pivoted and slipped through the narrow space between the office and the warehouse wall, moving with a suddenness that defied the sluggish appearance of his disguise. He found a low, recessed window—barely a slit—and peered inside. The room was dimly lit by a single, guttering oil lamp that cast long, dancing shadows across walls lined with overflowing filing cabinets and maps of the Skyrim coastline. The three men were huddled in a tight circle, their faces illuminated from below, giving them the appearance of conspirators in a dim cathedral of commerce.

Elias’s eyes locked onto the target: a heavy iron lockbox bolted to the floor beside the desk. That was where the true ledgers lived—the ones that wouldn't appear in any legal audit. He felt the cold weight of the mission pressing against him. To get to that box, he would have to bypass the pacing guard and the two men whose voices were now rising in a heated argument about a shipment of "refined salts" that had gone missing near the border. The tension in the room was palpable, a fragile equilibrium that could be shattered by the slightest noise.
