He could still feel the phantom weight of the guard's fist against his face, a reminder that the price of this "performance" had been paid in bone and blood.

The Bee and the Barb was a sanctuary of amber light and the smell of roasted malt, but to Elias, it currently felt like a gilded trap. His attic room offered a roof and a lock, but locks in Riften were merely suggestions to those with the right tools or a high enough bribe. He could almost hear Keerava’s voice, a silk-wrapped blade, reminding him that he was a guest on borrowed time and that "comfort" was a luxury the city rarely granted to strangers. Talen-Jei might have a shred of honor left in her veins, but honor didn't pay the taxes on a tavern, and Vinius’s coin was undoubtedly heavier than a stranger's gratitude.

He weighed the risk of the attic against the grueling reality of the streets. To return to the inn was to place himself in a known coordinate, a fixed point on a map for any guard who knew how to ask the right questions. The alternative was a nomadic vigil: blending into the kaleidoscope of the festival, drifting from the honey-nut vendors to the wine-stalls, never occupying the same patch of cobblestone for more than ten minutes. It was a recipe for exhaustion, a slow attrition of the nerves that would leave him frayed by dawn, but it was the only plan that didn't involve trusting someone else's discretion.

His options were narrowing, the window of opportunity closing like a fist. He stepped toward a public fountain, the water shimmering under the moonlight, and began the grim process of scrubbing the blood from his face. The copper scent clung to him, a neon sign to any guard with a keen nose. As he worked the water into his skin, he watched the crowds. The festival was peaking; the air was thick with the scent of cinnamon and sweat, and the music had shifted into a manic, driving tempo that made the very ground vibrate.

The water of the fountain was cold, but it couldn't wash away the lingering sense of being hunted. As Elias leaned over the basin, his mangled nose—still throbbing with a rhythmic, dull heat—suddenly flared. A thick, putrid odor collided with his senses, a visceral punch of fermented sewage and ancient fish guts that drowned out the sweetness of the festival’s cinnamon. It was a smell he recognized from the darker corners of the Empire's port cities: the scent of a place where the sun never reached. The stench was drifting up from the lip of the wooden bridge, leaking from the subterranean maw of the Ratway.

In Riften, the Ratway was more than just a sewer; it was a sanctuary for the discarded. It was where the desperate slunk off to shed the gaze of the law and the judgment of the nobility, a damp, echoing labyrinth where the only currency was silence and the only law was survival. For a man whose face was currently a map of bruises and blood, the Ratway offered a luxury the Bee and the Barb could not: anonymity. To return to the inn was to present himself as a stationary target; to descend into the tunnels was to become part of the city's hidden, pulsing vein.

He didn't linger. With one last glance at the glittering chaos of the square, Elias slipped away from the fountain and melted toward the bridge. He found a narrow, grime-streaked access point, a gap in the masonry where the runoff from the fisheries pooled into a stagnant, iridescent sludge. He slid down the slick stone, his boots splashing into ankle-deep filth that smelled of salt and decay, and felt the temperature drop ten degrees as the festive warmth of the city was replaced by a damp, oppressive chill.

The transition was absolute. Above him, the muffled thrum of the Festival of Love continued, a distant, ghostly melody that felt as though it belonged to another world entirely. Down here, the only sounds were the steady drip of limestone leaks and the occasional, frantic scuttle of vermin. Elias leaned his back against a weeping wall of moss, closing his eyes and letting his breath slow. He could feel the adrenaline receding, leaving behind a hollow ache in his chest and a sharp, pulsing reminder of the guard's fist.

Elias did not venture deep into the humid gloom of the Ratway. The bowels of the city were a siren song for the desperate, but he knew that the further one descended, the more the shadows began to bite. Instead, he carved out a perimeter on the outskirts, occupying that narrow, grey sliver of existence where the city’s filth met the subterranean silence. It was a tactical middle ground: tucked away from the prying eyes of the surface dwellers who occasionally peered down the grates, yet distant enough from the core of the sewers to avoid the predatory gaze of the vagrants and opportunists who claimed the deeper tunnels as their sovereign territory.

In this damp half-light, the reality of his situation settled over him with a cold, heavy weight. He had never felt more precarious. The ragged mercenary’s tunic he wore offered as much protection as a wet sheet of parchment, and the absence of his sword—left behind in the rush to maintain his disguise—felt like a missing limb. He was a warrior stripped of his steel, a spellsword without his focus, standing in a place where the only thing cheaper than life was the promise of a stranger.

A fleeting thought crossed his mind: the alley behind the fishery. His proper gear, his blade, and his dignity were still there, discarded like yesterday's refuse. But the thought was quickly dismissed by the logic of survival. To trek back across the city would mean crossing the heart of the festival, exposing his bruised face and blood-streaked clothes to the torchlight of the guards. He was a ghost in the shadows; to step back into the light now would be to invite the very handcuffs he had just leaped through a window to avoid.

He drifted toward a flicker of orange light—a makeshift fire fueled by driftwood and dried sewage-reeds, casting long, dancing silhouettes against the weeping stone. Around it huddled a small cluster of the city's forgotten: skooma addicts, their eyes wide and shimmering with a frantic, iridescent hunger, their bodies trembling in a rhythmic, involuntary dance. They watched him approach with a mixture of suspicion and primal curiosity, their gaze darting from his face to his throat, calculating his value in scraps.

Elias didn’t lead with a greeting; words were a luxury for those with something to bargain with. Instead, he stepped into the circle of heat and held out his waterskin. It was barely a third full, the liquid lukewarm and tasting of cured leather, but in the arid desperation of the Ratway, it was a currency of survival. He passed it to a woman whose skin had turned a translucent, ghostly grey, her fingers trembling so violently she nearly dropped the flask. She drank with a feral intensity, then passed it to the man beside her, and so it went—a silent, rhythmic communion of the wretched. For a while, Elias simply leaned against the damp stone, his eyes fixed on the flickering orange heart of the fire. There was a strange, heavy comfort in the silence here. Among these broken things, he didn't have to be a warrior or a disgraced vigilant; he was just another discarded soul, a smudge of shadow in a city of gold.

As the warmth seeped into his bruised ribs, his thoughts drifted toward Louis Letrush. They had agreed to reunite at a small, unassuming farm on the outskirts of the city stables, a neutral ground where the payout would change hands. A sharp, cold flicker of regret ignited in his chest. He had played the part of the trusting soldier, forgetting that in Riften, trust was a flaw in one's armor. He should have demanded half the gold up front. Letrush was a man whose soul was a series of revolving doors; he had no allegiance to anyone but the mirror.

Still, Elias allowed himself a moment of grace. He didn't want to believe that every hand extended in this city was merely feeling for a pocket to pick. But as he watched the embers pop and fade, the pragmatist in him began to calculate. If Letrush vanished with the coin, Elias had a final, jagged card to play: Maul. Maven Black-Briar’s watchdog didn't care for the morality of a heist, but he cared deeply about the geography of power. A schemer operating a sophisticated game of theft and betrayal within the city walls—especially one as flamboyant as Letrush—was a loose thread that the Black-Briars would want clipped.

The ledger might be a treasure to the thief, but the *existence* of the thief was a liability to the regime. Information on a rogue operator was a commodity that the Thieves Guild or Maven herself would pay well to acquire. He knew his own position in this equation was precarious; a hired blade was a dime a dozen in Riften, a replaceable tool in a city of hammers. But a man who knew where the bodies were buried—and who had helped dig the holes—was slightly more valuable than a common thug.

The fire dwindled to a collection of glowing ribs, pulsing like a dying heart in the damp dark. Elias didn't sleep so much as he drifted, his consciousness hovering in the shallow waters between exhaustion and alertness. He remained curled against the weeping limestone, one eye cracked open, watching the skooma-shadows lengthen and warp. Every time a distant splash echoed through the tunnel or a rat scurried across the sludge, his muscles tightened, a phantom reflex of a soldier who had spent too many nights sleeping with one hand on a hilt. He allowed himself only a few fragmented hours of oblivion, the kind of shallow rest that serves as a bridge rather than a destination, storing just enough strength to keep his legs from buckling when the time came to move.

As the others drifted into the heavy, slack-jawed slumber of the addicted, Elias felt the cold seep back into his marrow. The warmth of the driftwood fire had been a mercy, but mercy was a dangerous thing to rely on in the Ratway. He stood slowly, his joints popping with a rhythmic, metallic snap, and felt the bridge of his nose throb in time with his heartbeat. He didn't look back at the huddle of broken people; their silence was the only payment they could offer. With a final, lingering glance at the grey ceiling of the sewer, he began the slow, cautious ascent, timing his movements to the rhythm of the city's waking breath.

He emerged from the subterranean maw just as the sky over Riften began to bleed a pale, bruised lavender. The air was crisp, smelling of pine needles and the dying embers of the festival's torches. He moved like a smudge of charcoal against the cobblestones, slipping back toward the fishery with a predator's economy of motion. The alley was exactly as he had left it: silent, smelling of salt and old scales, and holding the remnants of his former self. He reached into the shadows and retrieved his gear, the familiar weight of his broken leather armor settling over his shoulders like a long-forgotten promise. He slid his sword back into its scabbard with a muted *click*, the sound grounding him. The thug was gone; the spellsword had returned.

The walk to the outskirts was a blur of morning mist and early-rising merchants. Elias bypassed the main thoroughfares, carving a path through the dew-slicked grass of the city fringes, his eyes fixed on the silhouette of the stables, the rendezvous not far beyond, and the promise of payment that would make the long night worth the all the trouble and excitement.
