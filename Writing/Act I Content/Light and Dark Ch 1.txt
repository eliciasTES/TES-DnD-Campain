Each heartbeat felt like a hammer against his ribs, a visceral reminder that he was gambling his life on the hope that a cultist’s greed outweighed their curiosity. He forced his breathing to shallow, to become a ghost of a rhythm, mimicking the slow, fading pulse of a man whose light was nearly spent.

The silence that followed was not empty; it was predatory. It was the kind of silence that felt like a physical pressure against the eardrums, a void that sucked the warmth from the air. He could feel the perimeter of the camp shifting, the same oppressive density he had sensed days ago now folding inward. The woods were no longer screaming; they were whispering, the sound of soft boots on damp loam signaling that the lure had worked, but the trap had also closed. He was no longer a man; he was a landmark of failure, a discarded husk left behind for the winners to find.

Somewhere to the east, a sharp, discordant cry ripped through the canopy—a scream of metal on stone or perhaps a voice cut short by a blade. It was the sound of Virel engaging the world, a signal that the dance had begun. Elias closed his eyes, letting the cold seep into his marrow, blurring the line between the frozen earth and his own skin. He didn't know if she was leading them into an ambush or simply running for her life, but the sheer audacity of their plan felt like the only honest thing he had done in years.

As the first few shadows of the hunters began to bleed into the clearing, Elias surrendered to the mask. He let his muscles go slack, his jaw drop slightly, and his consciousness drift into a grey, numb haze. He became the corpse he had promised to be, a silent witness to the arrival of the things that hunted him. The air grew thick with the smell of old copper and wet earth, and he felt the sudden, jarringness of a presence hovering just inches above him, a cold breath stirring the fine hairs on his cheek.
